## Release notes

### The future of AI comes into focus—and benchmarks are key

With each round of announcements, we’re getting a clearer picture of the future—and realizing just how important it is to keep careful tabs on what these models can (and can’t) do. This week industry heavyweights Anthropic and OpenAI sent detailed AI action plans to the U.S. government, signaling where each company is placing its bets. Elsewhere, there were major advancements and spirited debates about how we benchmark and understand these powerful tools. Let’s get you caught up, then we’ll break it all down.

### This week’s major developments

#### AI action plans: Anthropic and OpenAI set their lanes

-
[In its plan](https://assets.anthropic.com/m/4e20a4ab6512e217/original/Anthropic-Response-to-OSTP-RFI-March-2025-Final-Submission-v3.pdf)**, **Anthropic emphasized national security and model safety, calling AI a “critical national asset,” in part because, “Powerful AI technology will be built during this Administration.”

-
[OpenAI’s plan](https://cdn.openai.com/global-affairs/ostp-rfi/ec680b75-d539-4653-b297-8bcf6e5f7686/openai-response-ostp-nsf-rfi-notice-request-for-information-on-the-development-of-an-artificial-intelligence-ai-action-plan.pdf) focused on broad economic growth, proposing a comprehensive framework that ensures AI-driven prosperity and “freedom of intelligence,” arguing: “AI could drive significant increased productivity over the next decade.”

- Both strongly advocate for investing in infrastructure, workforce development, and stricter export controls to maintain the U.S.’s AI leadership over competitors like China.

#### Google’s robotics upgrade

Google’s Gemini 2.0 Flash now [natively handles images](https://developers.googleblog.com/en/experiment-with-gemini-20-flash-native-image-generation/). For the first time, Gemini can edit photos via chat, and generally has a much better understanding of pictures and video than prior models. I made the below image with the prompt:

> “Can you turn half of this photo of a headshot of a man in glasses into a hand-drawn style, like the image of the girl? The man should be split down the middle, with the left half being photorealistic with a black background, and the right half being a hand-drawn drawing of him in a minimal single-line style with black marker (his glasses, hair, shirt, and tie) on a white background. Keep everything on the left half the same.”

#### Optical character recognition (OCR) takes on new importance

-
[Mistral launched](https://mistral.ai/news/mistral-ocr) an OCR model claiming industry-leading accuracy for extracting information from PDFs, graphs, and handwritten documents—critical given an OCR market [worth tens of billions](https://www.grandviewresearch.com/industry-analysis/optical-character-recognition-market), as well as troves of valuable data locked in PDFs.

- LlamaIndex quickly [challenged](https://x.com/jerryjliu0/status/1898037050185859395) Mistral’s claims with benchmarks suggesting its workflows perform better, highlighting an ongoing—and necessary—debate around accurate measurement that extends beyond just document understanding tasks.

#### Developer tools

- Anthropic [released](https://docs.anthropic.com/en/docs/build-with-claude/tool-use/text-editor-tool) a text editor tool, simplifying iterative AI edits to files making it easier to build a “Cursor for X.”

- OpenAI [introduced](https://openai.com/index/new-tools-for-building-agents/) “new tools for building agents” in the form of updated APIs and SDKs.

#### Why this matters (and what’s next):

OpenAI and Anthropic are continuing to pick their lanes, as I reported [last week](https://every.to/context-window/we-gave-gpt-4-5-a-myers-briggs-test-it-s-an-extrovert)—Anthropic doubling down on safety and specialized applications like coding, while OpenAI aims for mass adoption and broad economic impact. There are three areas in which the companies agree, though:

- It is imperative the U.S. maintains global AI leadership against adversaries like China.

- Broad AI adoption will lead to economic prosperity, but investment in infrastructure and workforce development is a must.

- Very powerful AI is here, more is coming, and preparing for this is a priority for national security. These models are already powerful enough to change our world and are only getting better.

Taken at face value, these statements are alarming. The only way to understand how true they are is by objectively measuring what these models can (or can’t!) do—and doing that often. Benchmarks!
This week's debate over Mistral’s OCR illustrates how valuable—and challenging—it is to measure AI performance. OCR isn't niche: It impacts critical sectors like finance, healthcare, and government, where reliably pulling information from documents directly adds value. Neither LlamaIndex nor Mistral began with OCR, though: Llamaindex started with tools to make coding with language models easier, while Mistral trained models. They both gravitated toward the space for a simple reason: It’s a huge revenue opportunity, and return on investment is key. Each team's ability to perform better on benchmarks now has very real stakes, and both are incentivized to publish their own benchmarks where they do best.

##### A final note on benchmarks

Two great examples of creative approaches to testing model performance were released this week:

- Jina AI [shows](https://jina.ai/news/long-context-embedding-models-are-blind-beyond-4k-tokens/) that models get worse when you give them too long of a prompt. This is why no one has solved the problem of “chatting with your data”—if you put too much information into a prompt, performance starts to degrade.

- Anthropic adopted another game as a benchmark with [Factorio](https://jackhopkins.github.io/factorio-learning-environment/).

But what will truly identify the best model for you is the benchmark you make yourself. Compare model responses on the same prompt in OpenAI’s brand new [prompt playground](https://platform.openai.com/playground/chat) and see which model you like best. Why?
All benchmarks are undergirded with the belief that AI is transforming our digital world, but Google’s robotics advances hint at the next leap: bringing AI’s disruptive impact into the physical world. By using models that understand visual contexts directly (like Gemini 2.0 Flash), robots become not just smarter but significantly more adaptable to real-world environments. Check out some videos of these robots in action:

- [“Dunking” a basketball](https://x.com/TheHumanoidHub/status/1900262977582096576)

- [Spelling with Scrabble tiles](https://x.com/WevolverApp/status/1900155739802411225)

- [Folding origami](https://x.com/GoogleDeepMind/status/1899839627139383762)

Google is seeing substantial improvements: Gemini Robotics more than doubled performance on its open-source benchmark of 400 multiple choice questions that rely on vision, spatial reasoning, and task reasoning, among other things. If digital AI has made knowledge and creativity dramatically cheaper, physical AI—robotics—might do something even more remarkable: lower the barriers to transforming our built environment.—[Alex Duffy](https://every.to/@AlxAi)

He was a Holocaust survivor who built Commodore into a computing empire through sheer force of will and ruthless business tactics. His “religion” of vertical integration and relentless cost-cutting created the Commodore 64 computer, which sold an astonishing 12 million units in 12 years and launched countless tech careers by making computers affordable for working-class families. ## Sentiment analysis

***Gareth Edwards****’s chronicle of *[Jack Tramiel and the history of Commodore](https://every.to/the-crazy-ones/the-first-king-of-home-computing)* triggered the memories of a flood of people who grew up with the company’s computers. Here are just a few:*

> “To a working-class kid trying to work my way through college, that $120 computer and an old $5 TV as a monitor opened a whole new world for me. Learned ML, then assembly, then eventually went on to get a CS degree and a tech career.”

> "My mother was a secretary and my father worked in a factory. There's no way we could afford an Apple II or an IBM PC. That Commodore 64, though—that was the best $100 they ever invested in my future."

> “So much has been said about Steve Jobs and yet I almost never hear anything about Tramiel. Both were apparently absolutely ruthless businessmen, but I will always respect Tramiel’s ‘for the masses, not the classes’ approach to computers.”

> “So many Mountain Dew-soaked weekend nights dialing into local BBS's to chat with a stranger or download a game. We felt like true futuristic hackers in the late eighties on our Commodore 64.”

> “The first computer I ever encountered (aged 10) was a PET. I was hooked, and I absolutely loved my VIC-20. Amazing what could be done with just 3.5KB of RAM! Jack Tramiel changed my world.”

## Alignment

**Likes don't matter. **Performing for attention is soul-crushing work, which is why I wish I'd found [Arthur Brooks](https://arthurbrooks.com/)'s framework for creativity sooner. In the [Atlantic](https://www.theatlantic.com/ideas/archive/2025/03/true-artist-creativity-happiness/681997/), Brooks describes his three-question checklist before publishing: Does my work glorify the divine, does it uplift others, and is it personally interesting? I think this simple process prevents most people's worst creative impulses: publishing rage-bait for easy clicks, creating shallow content just to maintain visibility, sharing half-formed thoughts to seem relevant. I've since adapted it for my own checklist: Is it truthful? Does it serve beyond myself? Is it meaningful to me? The hierarchy matters—truth before service, service before self-interest. Rather than limiting creativity, I’ve found these boundaries focus it. I measure success on my terms, not on likes and shares. This way I become more authentically myself, creating from a place of integrity rather than performing like a circus animal for digital bananas.—[Ashwin Sharma](https://x.com/Ashwinreads)
