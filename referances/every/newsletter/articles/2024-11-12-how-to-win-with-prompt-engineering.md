Prompt engineering isn’t just about telling AI to solve your problems—it’s about knowing which ones to solve.

Yet there’s a mismatch between the people who can identify the right problems—experts with deep domain knowledge—and the technical infrastructure required for developing and refining prompts. [Jared Zoneraich](https://x.com/imjaredz), the cofounder and CEO of prompt engineering platform [PromptLayer](https://www.promptlayer.com/), is bridging the gap with a platform on which non-technical experts can manage, deploy, and evaluate prompts quickly.

The role of human prompt engineers, however, has been the topic of controversy, with [some](https://spectrum.ieee.org/prompt-engineering-is-dead) arguing that AI can optimize prompts better than us, while [others](https://web.archive.org/web/20230108000408/https://greylock.com/greymatter/sam-altman-ai-for-the-next-era/) suggest that more capable LLMs eliminate the need for meticulously crafted prompts altogether. I spent an hour talking to Jared about why he believes prompt engineering isn’t becoming obsolete. He also tells me everything he’s learned about writing a good prompt and what the future of AI tools looks like.
