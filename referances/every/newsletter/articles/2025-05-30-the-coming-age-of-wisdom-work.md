## Alignment

**Trillion-dollar temple.** Anxiety-inducing premiums, ticking-clock diagnoses, and a pandemic hangover have pushed many of us to seize the one variable still within reach: our own bodies. That grab for agency has ballooned the wellness economy to [$6.3 trillion](https://www.theatlantic.com/books/archive/2025/05/how-health-became-luxury-commodity/682957/), larger than Germany’s GDP and nearly four times the market capitalization of Big Pharma. In her book *[How to Be Well](https://www.amazon.com/How-Be-Well-Without-Spending/dp/0525655530)*, journalist **[Amy Larocca](https://www.instagram.com/amylaroccaauthor/)** tracks the consumer exodus from crowded clinics to infrared saunas and whisper-quiet spin classes. The boom looks convincingly commercial, yet I think that all of these studios behave like pop-up temples because inside, three instincts find refuge. First comes control: Heart-rate screens blink proof that sweat equals progress. Then self-reinvention: Mirrors let effort rehearse a better self. Finally, belonging: A nod or quick pat on the back binds strangers into a fragile tribe. The ritual costs $99 a month (towels are extra, of course), but people keep paying because it may be the last ritual they trust. And until other institutions offer a stronger liturgy, the wellness studio remains the last ceremony millions are willing to believe in.—*[Ashwin Sharma](https://x.com/Ashwinreads)*
