#### Knowledge Partner: McKinsey & Company

**Combating the Great Resignation.** Pop quiz: what’s the top reason people leave their job?

1. The pay

2. The benefits

3. A feeling of being undervalued, and sense of not belonging in an organization

Most managers pay attention to #1 and and #2 on the list, but McKinsey research finds that employees typically leave their jobs when they feel like they don’t belong, don’t have enough autonomy, and don’t like their team.

So what makes a workplace the kind of place that employees want to stay? A recent episode of [The McKinsey Podcast explains](https://mckinsey.com/Business-Functions/People-and-Organizational-Performance/Our-Insights/From-the-great-attrition-to-the-great-adaptation).

## [Notes on ConstitutionDAO](https://every.to/divinations/notes-on-constitutiondao)

[![](https://d24ovhgu8s7341.cloudfront.net/uploads/editor/posts/1940/optimized_56433CB6-ED2D-4AEA-BFCA-3B5FF9C2599C.jpeg)](https://every.to/divinations/notes-on-constitutiondao)

*Nathan Baschez / Divinations*
ICYMI this week (because maybe you were on a social media cleanse or living in a cave in the woods or something?), [ConstitutionDAO](https://twitter.com/ConstitutionDAO) took over Twitter. The goal of the DAO? Pool money to buy a copy of the US Constitution at the Sotheby's auction so it would be owned "by the people, for the people" rather than a single private collector.
Unfortunately, it didn't quite work. But it certainly made waves, raised over $40m in under a week, and gave us a whole collection of [Nicholas Cage](https://twitter.com/nbashaw/status/1459743899086471174?s=20) memes. Nathan's piece helps us see some of the key takeaways after this whirlwind of a week, along with this golden nugget of advice: "It sucks in the moment, but it is a necessary and valuable experience. Failure is progress. It all depends on what happens next."
[Read.](https://every.to/divinations/notes-on-constitutiondao)

## [Reddit’s missing business model: Embedding commerce in culture](https://every.to/p/reddit-s-missing-business-model-embedding-commerce-in-culture)

[![](https://d24ovhgu8s7341.cloudfront.net/uploads/editor/posts/1940/optimized_reddit.png)](https://every.to/p/reddit-s-missing-business-model-embedding-commerce-in-culture)

*Rohit Kaul / Every*
How can Reddit be so culturally powerful but simultaneously financially insignificant? In a guest post this week, Rohit Kaul argues that Reddit's reliance on ads for monetization (they are on track for $[350M](https://www.theinformation.com/articles/reddits-ad-revenue-expected-to-double-to-at-least-350-million-this-year?rc=yrmswp) this year) is hindering their growth. Because Reddit ads are primarily for awareness rather than purchase conversion, they end up competing against Meta, which has far superior targeting. Instead of trying to steal ad dollars, Rohit writes that Reddit should pursue “embedding commerce in culture” by giving its creators new monetization tools.
[Read.](https://every.to/p/reddit-s-missing-business-model-embedding-commerce-in-culture)

## [How Oatly Wins: Stories that Create Binding Commitments](https://every.to/masterful-storytelling/how-oatly-wins-stories-that-create-binding-commitments)

[![](https://d24ovhgu8s7341.cloudfront.net/uploads/editor/posts/1940/optimized_newsprint_oatly.jpeg)](https://every.to/masterful-storytelling/how-oatly-wins-stories-that-create-binding-commitments)

*Adam Davidson/ Masterful Storytelling*
In his debut article for Every, Adam Davidson (co-founder of NPR's Planet Money) lays out the argument that we are leaving the age of branding and entering the age of storytelling as a marketing strategy. Using Oatly as his primary example, Adam shows us how storytelling can help companies appeal to a niche—but more dedicated— audience. "Once understood, [storytelling] creates a degree of passionate engagement that more traditional approaches can’t achieve."
We're so excited to have Adam writing with us, and this piece makes a compelling case for throwing out the traditional branding playbook.

## [Say “I” not “We” when speaking to customers](https://tips.ariyh.com/p/say-i-not-we-to-customers)

[![](https://d24ovhgu8s7341.cloudfront.net/uploads/editor/posts/1940/optimized_https___bucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com_public_images_09f18c0d-f747-4371-b374-120ea43cf482_1840x962.png)](https://tips.ariyh.com/p/say-i-not-we-to-customers)

*Thomas McKinlay / Ariyh*
Current ‘best practices’ in customer interactions are based on a [1982 study](https://doi.org/10.1177/002224378201900307) that says company agents should downplay their “*self”* and emphasize the “*firm*.” However, more recent studies show that you should do exactly the opposite to increase customer satisfaction.
There is a lot of marketing advice on the internet, but very little is research-based, or even *good* advice. So how do sift through the junk? That's where Thomas McKinlay's newsletter [Ariyh](https://ariyh.com/) comes in. Ariyh gives you 3-minute marketing tips based on actual scientific research from the top business schools, such as [saying "thank you" instead of "sorry"](https://tips.ariyh.com/p/thanks-not-sorry) and [showing your costs can increase sales by 15-20%](https://tips.ariyh.com/p/show-costs-to-boost-sales).
[Read it here.](https://ariyh.com/)
