## Alignment

**Freedom to discover. **When we think of the cutting edge of medicine, we think of miracle drugs, dramatic surgeries, and devices that prolong our lives. But ask any researcher and they’ll tell you: The path to each breakthrough is paved with paperwork.
That’s why the pharma giant Novo Nordisk’s new use case for Claude is so impressive—Anthropic’s AI was able to cut the creation of clinical documentation [from 10 weeks to 10 minutes](https://www.ft.com/content/b9c6b9d0-c418-45b1-a266-72605ccc8aaa?accessToken=zwAGQe5ipFZYkdO5xrnQxBhFsdOiZnJgXMyKqg.MEYCIQClgC5EGbSdtf1-MISKIAxNLKEpVVa55e6DPRl67CXSqQIhAIgU2U8jrmav1M0RWadfrYwfFHuisUh4oaoKIBclAeTp&sharetype=gift&token=a7cd728b-65b6-4e0f-b1ba-f40b42520d2c).
Right now, a single clinical trial requires thousands of pages of documentation recording every side effect, every dose delivered, and every deviation from protocol. That data then has to be custom-formatted to meet a litany of varying standards set by regulators and institutional review boards. It adds up to about two and a half months of a researcher's life spent describing what they did instead of doing science.
By embedding Claude directly into lab systems, Anthropic is giving scientists what coders already have: tools that automate tedious documentation and streamline their workflow.
Of course, we'll still need to watch for AI hallucinations, and yes, there's still huge value in researchers spending time writing as a means of processing their own thoughts. But killing off mountains of paperwork is an unmitigated net good that will free up scientists to spend more time discovering the next life-saving breakthrough.—*[Ashwin Sharma](https://www.glp1digest.com/)*
